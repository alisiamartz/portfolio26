<h2 id="overview">Overview</h2>

Lift is a VR interactive narrative experience originally built for HTC Vive, later expanded toward other headsets. You play an elevator operator in an old hotel, and the storyline changes depending on how you treat and respond to guests.

The project began as a university pitch selected from a large pool of ideas and developed by a full team, then continued beyond graduation as an ongoing studio project.

<h2 id="challenge">Challenge</h2>

Immersive mechanics don’t automatically translate to accessible mechanics. Early interaction concepts sometimes required too much physical effort, had unclear affordances, or didn’t scale well across different player setups and bodies.

We needed interactions that still felt tactile and “VR-native,” while being reliable, comfortable, and easy to understand.

<h2 id="approach">Approach</h2>

- Build interactions like props: clear affordances, satisfying feedback, minimal ambiguity.
- Prototype quickly, then harden into stable systems once the feel is right.
- Preserve delight while improving accessibility (translate the gesture instead of removing the reward).

<h2 id="process">Process</h2>

**Wearables (hats + badge)**  
Players loved “wearing” objects, so I implemented wearables early and used playtests to understand how players expressed personality through them (e.g., tipping a hat to an NPC).

**Elevator floor lever**  
A side-to-side lever motion created a distinctive physical interaction and clear visual feedback. Implementation uncovered angular drift issues; I replaced reliance on a generic toolkit approach for this object with direct calculations based on hand position to keep the lever aligned and believable.

**Door opening iteration**  
An early door-opening mechanic required bending and lifting, which was high-effort and not accessible. We explored alternatives and iterated toward an approach that reduced effort while keeping the “mechanical” satisfaction of operating an elevator.

<h2 id="outcome">Outcome</h2>

Lift evolved from a student project into a long-running studio effort with a deeper narrative focus. The interaction systems I built became the foundation for continued iteration as the story and moment-to-moment guest interactions expanded.
